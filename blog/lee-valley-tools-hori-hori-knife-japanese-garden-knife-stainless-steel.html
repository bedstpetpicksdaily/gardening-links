Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Lee Valley hori hori compare to other Japanese garden knives?

The main difference is material consistency and edge retention. Cheaper hori horis use lower-grade stainless or carbon steel that dulls noticeably after 4-6 weeks of regular use. Lee Valley's blade stays sharp 2-3 times longer because the steel is higher carbon content with better heat treatment. You'll notice this in July when you're doing heavy summer maintenance—the blade glides through soil instead of requiring downward pressure. That said, basic hori horis ($12-18) work fine if you sharpen them monthly or don't mind replacing them annually.

Is the graduated ruler actually useful, or is it just a gimmick?

It's genuinely useful if you plant anything with specific depth requirements. Garlic cloves need 2 inches exactly, potatoes need 4 inches, most transplants need 6-8 inches. Without markings, you're eyeballing or measuring every single planting, which adds five minutes per dozen transplants. With the ruler, you mark your target depth mentally once, then work rhythmically. For seed potatoes in July (if you're doing succession planting in cooler regions), this becomes your standard reference point all season.

Will stainless steel really prevent rust better than carbon steel?

Yes, but with caveats. Stainless steel resists rust far better, but it's not rust-proof—it'll still stain if left wet or exposed to chlorinated water for extended periods. The real advantage is that you can be less obsessive about immediate cleaning. Forget to rinse your hori hori after watering? Stainless won't develop rust spots overnight. Carbon steel hori horis will. If you're organized and always clean tools immediately, the practical difference shrinks. If you're realistic about tool maintenance (which most of us are), stainless saves frustration.
