Best Seeds & Plants for Container Gardening: A Comparative Guide

Container gardening appeals to balcony dwellers, small-space gardeners, and anyone without in-ground garden beds. But not every plant thrives in a pot—success depends on matching the right variety to the right container size, soil depth, and your climate.

This guide compares the major categories of seeds and plants that actually perform well in containers: leafy greens, herbs, tomatoes, peppers, beans, and root vegetables. We've broken down root depth requirements, maturation timelines, container sizes, and yield potential so you can choose what fits your space and growing timeline.

Leafy Greens (Lettuce, Spinach, Kale, Arugula)

Leafy greens are the fastest-maturing, lowest-maintenance container option. Lettuce, spinach, arugula, and kale require only 6–8 inches of soil depth and germinate within 7–10 days. They're ready to harvest in 30–45 days, making them ideal for succession planting—you can replant the same container multiple times per season.

These crops work in 12-inch diameter planters and adapt well to partial shade. They're also beginner-friendly and produce reliable harvests with minimal fertilizer once established.

Best for: Beginners, balcony gardeners, those wanting quick harvests and multiple plantings per season

Herbs (Basil, Parsley, Cilantro, Thyme, Rosemary, Mint)

Herbs are among the most economical container choices. Basil, parsley, cilantro, chives, oregano, and thyme need only 6–12 inches of soil depth and thrive in well-draining containers. Most Mediterranean herbs (rosemary, thyme, oregano) prefer moderate watering and well-draining soil, while mint is aggressive and benefits from larger containers to contain its growth.

Herbs produce abundant foliage throughout the growing season and can be harvested continuously, rewarding small seed investments with weeks of kitchen-ready crops. Positioning containers near your kitchen door encourages regular harvesting and bushier growth.

Best for: Home cooks, those wanting continuous harvests, balcony and patio gardeners

Determinate Tomatoes (Cherry and Bush Varieties)

Determinate (bush) tomato varieties significantly outperform indeterminate types in containers. Cherry and determinate cultivars thrive in 10–20 gallon containers with 8–12 inches of soil depth. They need 60–90 days to produce fruit and offer substantial yields from a compact footprint.

These varieties have naturally compact growth habits, making them ideal for patios and balconies. Container growing can actually improve productivity by controlling root space, and proper drainage is critical for success.

Best for: Tomato lovers with limited space, patio and balcony gardeners wanting reliable yields

Peppers (Sweet and Hot Varieties)

Both sweet and hot pepper varieties thrive in containers and often produce more abundantly when root space is controlled. Peppers need 8–12 inches of soil depth and 10–20 gallon containers. Like tomatoes, they require 60–90 days to produce fruit and benefit significantly from proper drainage.

Peppers are compact, visually attractive, and provide multiple harvests throughout the season. They're slightly less beginner-friendly than leafy greens but reward patient gardeners with steady production.

Best for: Medium-skill gardeners, those wanting longer production windows, patio gardeners wanting ornamental + edible appeal

Root Vegetables (Radishes, Carrots, Beets) & Beans

Root vegetables and beans occupy a middle ground: faster than tomatoes, deeper-rooted than lettuce. Radishes mature in 20–30 days in shallow containers, while carrots and beets need deeper planters and 50–70 days to maturity. Bush beans and peas produce well in 5–10 gallon containers within 50–70 days.

These crops work well for succession planting and offer reliable yields when container depth matches root depth. They're more forgiving than herbs about water consistency but require more space than leafy greens.

Best for: Gardeners wanting variety, those with moderate container space, succession planters

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Leafy greens germinate in 7–10 days and are harvestable in 30–45 days, enabling multiple plantings per season in the same container
  • ✓ Herbs require minimal soil depth (6–12 inches) and provide continuous harvests with small seed investments, ideal for kitchen-door access
  • ✓ Determinate tomatoes and peppers produce abundant yields in controlled 10–20 gallon containers despite compact growth, often outperforming larger varieties
  • ✓ Container gardening eliminates in-ground soil prep, weeding, and pest pressure, making it accessible for balcony, patio, and small-space gardeners
Cons
  • ✗ Shallow-rooted crops (leafy greens, herbs) require frequent watering in warm weather and can bolt or stress if moisture becomes inconsistent
  • ✗ Tomatoes and peppers need 60–90 days to fruit, longer than greens and herbs, and demand consistent nutrition and drainage management

Our Verdict

No single option wins across all use cases. If you want fast, beginner-friendly harvests and multiple crops per season, start with leafy greens and herbs—they mature in 30–60 days with minimal soil depth. If you have more patience and want substantial yields from limited space, determinate tomatoes and peppers justify the 60–90 day wait. For balance, combine shallow-rooted greens and herbs (6–8 inch pots) with medium-depth crops like beans and root vegetables (8–12 inch pots) in the same garden setup. Success hinges less on seed variety than on matching container depth to root depth, ensuring adequate drainage, and watering consistently. All five categories thrive in containers when these fundamentals align with your climate and available sunlight.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the minimum container size for each type?

Leafy greens and herbs: 6–8 inch depth, 12-inch diameter pots. Root vegetables and beans: 8–12 inch depth, 5–10 gallon containers. Tomatoes and peppers: 10–20 gallon containers, 8–12 inch depth. Larger containers always perform better than minimum sizes.

Can I grow indeterminate tomatoes in containers?

Indeterminate varieties are not recommended for containers. They grow 6–10 feet tall, require extensive staking, and struggle with the root constraints of pots. Determinate and cherry tomato varieties are bred for compact growth and far outperform indeterminate types in container settings.

How often should I water container plants?

Frequency varies by plant and climate. Leafy greens and herbs prefer consistent moisture but not waterlogged soil—water when the top inch of soil is dry. Tomatoes and peppers need deeper watering less frequently. Always ensure drainage holes are present; poor drainage kills more container plants than underwatering.

Can I succession plant in the same container?

Yes, especially with leafy greens and herbs. After harvesting lettuce or spinach (30–45 days), refresh the soil with compost and replant the same pot. Herbs can be harvested continuously and don't require replanting. Tomatoes and peppers occupy containers for 60–90+ days, so succession planting them is less practical in the same space.

What soil should I use?

Use quality potting mix (not garden soil) that retains moisture while draining well. Most successful container gardeners blend potting mix with compost for nutrients. Avoid heavy garden soil, which compacts and prevents drainage in pots.
