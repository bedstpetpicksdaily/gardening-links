Frequently Asked Questions

How long do self watering spikes keep plants watered?

Most self watering spikes can keep plants hydrated for 7-14 days depending on the spike size, plant type, and soil conditions. Larger reservoirs and slower-draining soil will extend watering duration, while small pots and fast-draining soil may require refilling sooner. For vacations longer than two weeks, you may need multiple spikes per plant or a different watering solution.

Do watering spikes work for all types of plants?

Self watering spikes work well for most houseplants including pothos, philodendrons, and ferns, but aren't ideal for succulents or cacti that prefer drier conditions. Plants that need consistent moisture between waterings benefit most from spikes. Avoid using them on plants that require completely dry periods between waterings, as the continuous moisture may cause root rot.

Can self watering spikes cause root rot or overwatering?

Self watering spikes release water gradually based on soil moisture, reducing overwatering risk compared to manual watering, but overwatering is still possible in pots with poor drainage. Using spikes with well-draining soil and drainage holes is essential to prevent root rot. If your plant's soil stays soggy, the spike may be delivering too much water or your drainage needs improvement.

How do you fill and use self watering spikes correctly?

Fill the spike's water globe or reservoir completely with water, insert it at an angle into moist soil about 2-3 inches deep, and ensure the spike sits firmly in the pot. The water will release slowly as the soil dries out; a vacuum forms that stops water flow when soil is wet enough. Before leaving, test the spike by checking soil moisture daily for 2-3 days to confirm it's delivering the right amount of water for your plant.
