Lechuza Cubico vs. Balconera Self-Watering Planters: Which Should You Buy?

By Best Gardening Picks Daily • July 20, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

Self-watering planters promise to solve the same problem: keeping plants alive when life gets busy. But not all self-watering systems are created equal, and price differences can be substantial. Lechuza, a European brand with genuine traction in the market, offers two distinct models that serve different needs—the compact Cubico Color 16" and the larger Balconera 80cm—yet many gardeners don't understand the real differences between them.

This isn't a review of one planter. It's a side-by-side comparison of two legitimate options, each with different sizing, pricing, and use cases. We've pulled real specs, pricing, and reviewer feedback to help you decide which (if either) makes sense for your space and budget.

Lechuza Cubico Color 16" Self-Watering Planter

The Cubico Color is Lechuza's compact, contemporary option. At 16 inches, it's sized for individual specimen plants or smaller groupings. The dual-chamber self-watering system keeps soil consistently moist for 2-3 weeks between reservoir fills. The Slate finish is intentionally polished, designed to look like furniture rather than garden equipment.

With over 500 reviews averaging 4.3 stars, it's got solid market validation. The air-pruning root system and integrated saucer prevent root rot, making it forgiving for plant experimentation. Price typically runs $40-70 depending on retailer and timing.

Best for: Small spaces, patios, contemporary décor, or gardeners who want a single statement planter without committing to a full container garden setup.

Lechuza Balconera 80cm Self-Watering Planter

The Balconera is the larger, deeper alternative—80cm translates to substantially more growing volume than the Cubico. Its self-watering reservoir extends time between waterings to 7-10 days even in peak summer heat, and the integrated water gauge removes guesswork about refill timing. Nearly 5,000 reviews with 4.5-star average rating suggest strong real-world reliability.

At roughly $10 (per source material), it's positioned as the budget-friendly entry to Lechuza's system. The UV-stable material resists fading and cracking over multiple seasons. Depth accommodates herbs, small shrubs, and shallow-rooted vegetables—not just trailing plants.

Best for: Balconies, larger patio spaces, gardeners growing multiple or larger plants, and anyone prioritizing value without sacrificing durability or function.

Budget Alternative: Fabric Pot with Saucer & Mulch Layer

The Cubico review itself mentions that a $15 fabric pot paired with a saucer and mulch layer will meaningfully reduce watering frequency without the premium price tag. This approach trades built-in water management for simplicity and cost savings. Mulch acts as moisture retention, and a saucer prevents overwatering from below.

This works if you're willing to monitor soil moisture occasionally and accept that you'll water more frequently than with a dual-chamber system. It's the pragmatic choice if Lechuza's pricing feels prohibitive.

Best for: Budget-conscious gardeners, renters, or anyone wanting to test self-watering concepts before investing in engineered systems.

Our Verdict

The Balconera wins on value and capacity. At roughly $10 with nearly 5,000 five-star reviews, it's positioned as the genuinely accessible entry to Lechuza's self-watering system. Its 80cm depth and 7-10 day watering interval make it practical for real balcony gardens—multiple plants, herbs, small shrubs. If you're growing anything beyond a single accent plant, the Balconera delivers more utility per dollar.

The Cubico wins on aesthetics and compactness. At 16 inches, it's the right choice for small spaces, contemporary interiors, or gardeners who want a single statement piece that doubles as décor. Its 2-3 week watering interval is genuinely convenient. The trade-off is price ($40-70) for a planter that holds one plant.

Neither beats a fabric pot + saucer + mulch setup if your only constraint is budget and you're willing to monitor soil occasionally. You'll water more frequently, but you'll save $25-60 and can reinvest in better soil or plant variety.

Choose Balconera for a productive balcony or patio garden. Choose Cubico if space or aesthetics are non-negotiable. Choose neither if you're testing the concept—a cheap fabric pot works fine until you know you need the engineered approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often do I actually refill the water reservoir?

Balconera extends time between waterings to 7-10 days in peak summer heat; Cubico extends to 2-3 weeks. Both include a water gauge indicator, so you fill when the gauge signals low rather than guessing. Actual frequency depends on plant type, soil mix, and heat—these are realistic ranges, not guarantees.

Is the price difference between Cubico ($40-70) and Balconera (~$10) worth it?

Not if you're comparing them directly. They're different products for different use cases. The Balconera is the better value for growing multiple plants; the Cubico is priced for its compact size and contemporary aesthetics. Compare Cubico to other 16-inch designer planters (~$50+), and the price becomes reasonable. Compare Balconera to basic plastic planters ($2-5), and it's a steal for durability and function.

Will the Slate finish show dirt and water stains?

Yes. Both models in Slate are attractive when clean but require periodic wiping to maintain that polished look. If you're in a dusty area or near hard water sources, expect visible staining. This is a trade-off for choosing aesthetics over pure practicality.

Can I use these indoors, or are they only for patios?

Both can be used indoors if you place them on a protected surface that can handle water. The integrated saucer on the Cubico helps, but the Balconera is deeper and heavier—indoors, it's more practical for patios, balconies, or covered porches. Neither is designed for windowsill use.

What plants work best in these planters?

Cubico (16"): Single specimen plants, shallow-rooted annuals, ornamentals. Balconera (80cm deep): Herbs, small shrubs, shallow-rooted vegetables, multiple smaller plants grouped together. Both work with any plant, but depth and volume determine what thrives long-term.

Do I need special soil, or will regular potting mix work?

Regular potting mix works fine. The Balconera review notes that proper setup of the bottom reservoir is what matters—fill it correctly on first use. Using quality potting soil (rather than garden soil) prevents compaction and ensures the air-pruning system functions as designed.
