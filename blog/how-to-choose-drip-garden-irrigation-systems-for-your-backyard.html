What to Look For

  • Water pressure compatibility: Backyard systems typically run on standard household water pressure (40-60 PSI), so look for drip systems rated for this range. If you have low pressure from a well or older plumbing, you'll need a pressure regulator or a system specifically designed for low-pressure applications. High-pressure systems will waste water and create uneven distribution.
  • Flexibility for mixed plantings: Your backyard likely has different plant types—some in raised beds needing consistent moisture, others in containers requiring more frequent watering. Choose a modular system with adjustable emitters and drip lines that let you customize water delivery for each zone. This flexibility is what separates "set and forget" systems from ones you'll constantly adjust.
  • Easy installation and maintenance: As a homeowner (not a landscaping pro), you want a system you can install yourself without special tools or expertise. Look for systems with simple connectors, clearly labeled components, and instructions that don't require a physics degree. Also consider how easy it is to unclog emitters and replace damaged sections—because clogs happen.

What to Avoid

  • Ultra-cheap kits with fixed emitters: The $15-20 systems with non-adjustable drip lines might seem like a bargain, but they deliver the same amount of water everywhere. Your shallow container plants will drown while your deep raised beds stay dry. Fixed systems also clog more easily and rarely come with decent pressure regulators, leading to leaks and frustration.
  • Soaker hoses as your main system: Soaker hoses feel like the easy choice, but they have uneven water distribution along their length (the end gets less water), they're not adjustable for different plant needs, and they degrade quickly in sunlight. They work okay for a single long row, but not for a diverse backyard garden layout.
  • Heavy-duty commercial systems: Professional-grade systems designed for large properties or farms

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What size drip irrigation system do I need for my garden?

    Measure your garden's square footage and calculate water needs based on your plants and soil type—most vegetable gardens need 1-2 inches of water per week. A general rule is to plan for 0.5-1 gallon per hour per emitter for typical backyard gardens, then multiply by the number of plants or linear feet of rows you're irrigating. Online calculators from irrigation suppliers can help you determine exact gallonage and system size needed.

    Should I choose drip tape or drip line for my garden?

    Drip tape is thinner, more affordable, and ideal for vegetable gardens or annual beds since it's typically replaced yearly, while drip line is thicker, more durable, and better for permanent installations around shrubs and perennials. Drip tape works well for row crops and close plant spacing, whereas drip line performs better with widely-spaced plants and rocky soil that might puncture tape.

    What's the difference between pressure-compensating and non-compensating drip emitters?

    Pressure-compensating emitters deliver consistent water flow across slopes and long runs despite variations in water pressure, making them ideal for uneven terrain or gardens longer than 200 feet. Non-compensating emitters are cheaper and work fine on flat ground with shorter runs, but water output decreases significantly at the end of long lines.

    Do I need a timer and filter for my drip irrigation system?

    A timer is highly recommended as it automates watering to early morning hours when evaporation is lowest and saves you from manual operation, with basic models costing $20-50. A filter is essential to prevent emitter clogs from sediment and debris in your water line—at minimum use a 150-200 micron screen filter before the main line.
