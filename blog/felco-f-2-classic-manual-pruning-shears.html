Frequently Asked Questions

How do Felco F-2s compare to cheaper bypass shears like Fiskars or Corona?

Fiskars ($15-20) offer decent cutting for casual use but the blade dulls faster and the action becomes stiff within a season. Corona ($20-25) are tougher but heavier, fatiguing your hand on longer pruning sessions. Felco F-2s split the difference—lighter than Corona, sharper longer than Fiskars, and with replacement parts available forever. If you prune more than 3-4 times monthly, the Felco advantage becomes tangible. For 3 times or fewer, save your money.

Do I really need Swiss-made pruning shears, or is that marketing?

Swiss manufacturing isn't marketing fluff here—it means tighter tolerances, better steel quality, and more rigid quality control. You'll notice it in the cutting action: the blade glides, doesn't stick or slip. That said, high-end Japanese options (Okatsune, ARS) match or exceed Felco's performance at similar prices. The difference is tradition and availability. Felco parts are everywhere. Japanese shears sometimes require mail-order replacements.

Will the F-2 actually last 20 years like people claim?

Yes, with realistic care. Don't leave them in wet soil, wipe them dry after use, and oil the pivot occasionally. Thousands of gardens have 10-15 year old Felco F-2s still cutting cleanly. However, heavy daily commercial use (nurseries, landscapers) might see blade wear in 5-7 years—though even then, you swap the blade for $8 and keep going. For home gardeners pruning a few hours weekly, 15-20 years is entirely realistic.

Is July the best time to buy these, or should I wait for sales?

July is actually the worst time to buy for deals—demand is highest during peak growing season. Wait until September-October when summer gardens wind down, and retailers discount pruning tools to clear inventory. You'll often find Felco F-2s at $35-45 instead of $50-60. The shears work the same either way, but your budget stretches further in fall.
