Best Outdoor Plants for Beginners: Perennials vs. Vegetables Compared

By Best Gardening Picks Daily • July 20, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

If you're new to gardening, choosing the right plants makes the difference between a thriving garden and one that withers from neglect. But "easy" means different things depending on what you want: colorful perennials that return year after year, productive vegetables you can harvest and eat, or low-maintenance herbs for cooking. This guide compares the actual best beginner plants across these categories—with honest pros and cons for each.

We've analyzed the characteristics that make plants forgiving: drought tolerance, pest resistance, adaptability to poor soil, and minimal watering needs. The key insight: different plants excel for different reasons. Sedums and ornamental grasses almost never die. Tomatoes and zucchini produce food quickly. Herbs like basil and mint multiply faster than you can use them. Understanding these distinctions helps you pick what actually suits your goals.

Sedums (Succulent Perennials)

Sedums are drought-tolerant succulents that store water in their leaves, requiring watering only once every 2–3 weeks. They thrive in full sun and poor soil, making them ideal for rocky areas, containers, or neglected corners. Available in dozens of varieties with different colors and textures, sedums establish quickly and spread naturally without fussing.

Best for: Gardeners who travel, forget to water, or want zero-maintenance color; rocky or well-draining soil areas; containers and raised beds.

Black-Eyed Susans (Rudbeckia)

Golden-yellow flowers that bloom reliably from summer through fall with minimal intervention. Black-eyed Susans are nearly impossible to kill, thrive in full sun with well-draining soil, and attract pollinators naturally. They spread to fill spaces and return year after year as hardy perennials, requiring no replanting.

Best for: Beginners wanting visual impact; pollinator gardens; full-sun borders; gardeners who want color without constant maintenance.

Daylilies and Coneflowers (Perennial Flowers)

Daylilies and coneflowers tolerate a wide range of soil types, handle temperature fluctuations, and bounce back from neglect. These hardy perennials establish within the first growing season and provide reliable blooms for years without replanting. Both adapt to various sunlight conditions and require minimal pruning or fertilizing.

Best for: Low-maintenance flower gardens; mixed borders; gardeners building confidence; zones with temperature swings.

Tomatoes (Vegetables—Cherry and Determinate Varieties)

Cherry and determinate varieties like 'Sungold' and 'Early Girl' produce fruit in 50–70 days, grow in containers or garden beds, and only need sunlight, water, and basic support. Tomatoes forgive irregular watering better than many vegetables and deliver visible, edible results quickly—turning beginners into gardeners with real accomplishment.

Best for: First-time gardeners wanting edible rewards; container gardening; limited space; those who want fast, tangible harvests.

Herbs (Basil, Mint, Rosemary)

Basil and mint are nearly impossible to kill, grow in average soil, and adapt to various sunlight conditions. Mint spreads so aggressively it often needs containment; basil produces abundant leaves for cooking within weeks. Rosemary is drought-tolerant once established. Herbs provide immediate culinary value and multiply faster than most beginners can use them.

Best for: Cooks wanting fresh ingredients; container gardeners; those who prefer edible plants; anyone wanting quick, visible growth.

Zucchini and Lettuce (Easy Vegetables)

Zucchini grows so vigorously that one plant feeds a family for months—the challenge becomes managing abundance rather than coaxing growth. Lettuce and leafy greens reach harvest size in 4–6 weeks, tolerate partial shade, and grow in cool or warm seasons. Both work in raised beds, containers, and are forgiving of beginner watering mistakes.

Best for: Vegetable gardeners; those wanting quick harvests; partial-shade areas; container and raised-bed gardens; seasonal eating.

Ornamental Grasses

Ornamental grasses tolerate neglect, thrive in various soil conditions, and require minimal watering once established. They provide year-round visual interest with texture and movement, don't demand frequent pruning, and adapt to most climates. Many varieties are native plants that support local ecosystems.

Best for: Low-maintenance borders; textural interest in gardens; erosion control; native plant enthusiasts; year-round visual appeal.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Perennials (sedums, black-eyed Susans, daylilies) return year after year without replanting—compound success over time.
  • ✓ Vegetables (tomatoes, zucchini, herbs) provide quick, visible, edible rewards within weeks—builds confidence and delivers real value.
  • ✓ All recommended options tolerate poor soil, irregular watering, and common beginner mistakes—forgiving of neglect.
  • ✓ Wide variety allows customization: choose perennials for permanent color, vegetables for food, herbs for cooking, or mix all three.
Cons
  • ✗ Perennials require patience: first-year establishment before reliable flowering; slower visible progress than vegetables.
  • ✗ Vegetables need more active management: staking (tomatoes), harvesting schedules (zucchini), seasonal replanting; not permanent solutions.
  • ✗ Mint spreads aggressively and can overtake gardens if not contained; requires deliberate control.
  • ✗ Sedums and drought-tolerant plants may appear sparse in year one before spreading; visual payoff is delayed compared to mature-size vegetables.

Our Verdict

The best choice depends on your priority. If you want permanent, low-maintenance color: start with sedums, black-eyed Susans, and ornamental grasses. These establish once and reward you with years of reliable blooms and foliage with minimal intervention—perfect if gardening feels like a chore and you just want beauty without fussing.

If you want fast, edible results: plant tomatoes, herbs (basil, mint), zucchini, and lettuce. These deliver visible harvests in weeks, teach you plant needs through hands-on watering and care, and build confidence through tangible accomplishment. They're ideal for cooks and those wanting immediate payoff.

The honest winner: plant both. Start perennials (sedums, daylilies, black-eyed Susans) in permanent beds to establish a no-fuss backbone, then add vegetables and herbs in containers or dedicated spaces for seasonal eating and faster victories. This hybrid approach gives you the best of both worlds: permanent structure that requires minimal attention, plus seasonal projects that keep gardening engaging and rewarding. All these options genuinely tolerate beginner mistakes—the real difference is whether you're building a garden or a kitchen, and how much time you want to invest each season.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the single easiest outdoor plant to start with?

Sedums and zucchini tie. Sedums are impossible to kill—they thrive on neglect and poor soil. Zucchini produces abundance so aggressively that failure is nearly impossible. Choose sedums if you want permanent, decorative plants; zucchini if you want food and fast growth.

Do all these plants really need minimal water?

Perennials (sedums, black-eyed Susans, daylilies, ornamental grasses) are genuinely drought-tolerant once established. Vegetables (tomatoes, zucchini, lettuce) and herbs need more consistent watering during growth, but tolerate irregular watering better than fussy plants. Mint and basil forgive skipped days more readily than, say, delicate flowers.

Can I grow these in containers?

Yes. Tomatoes, herbs (basil, mint, rosemary), lettuce, and zucchini thrive in containers with good drainage. Sedums and smaller perennials work well in pots. Larger perennials like black-eyed Susans and ornamental grasses need deeper containers but are absolutely possible. Container growing actually makes them *easier* for beginners because you control soil quality and watering.

Which plants actually come back every year?

Perennials: sedums, black-eyed Susans, daylilies, coneflowers, rosemary, and ornamental grasses all return annually. Vegetables (tomatoes, zucchini, lettuce) and annual herbs (basil) need replanting each season. Mint is perennial but spreads aggressively. Plan accordingly based on whether you want permanent plantings or seasonal rotation.

What if my garden gets partial shade?

Daylilies, hostas, and coneflowers adapt to partial shade. Lettuce and leafy greens actually prefer partial shade in warm climates. Mint tolerates shade well. Sedums, black-eyed Susans, tomatoes, and basil prefer full sun but some varieties handle dappled light. Check specific cultivar descriptions—many perennials list shade tolerance explicitly.

How long until I see results?

Vegetables and herbs: 4–8 weeks (lettuce 4–6 weeks, tomatoes 50–70 days, basil 3–4 weeks). Perennials: establish within the first growing season but don't reach full size/flower abundance until year two. This is why combining both gives fastest visible success—vegetables reward you immediately while perennials build lasting structure.
