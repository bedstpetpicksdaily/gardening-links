What to Look For

  • Drainage holes and water retention balance: Your seed tray needs adequate drainage to prevent damping-off disease, but it should also retain enough moisture so you're not watering constantly. Look for trays with multiple small drainage holes on the bottom and consider whether they come with humidity domes—these create a microclimate that reduces watering frequency while maintaining proper drainage.
  • Cell size appropriate to your seeds: Different seeds have different needs. Large seeds like beans and squash need deeper, larger cells (2-3 inches), while tiny seeds like petunias thrive in shallow cells. Standard 72-cell trays work well for most vegetables, but 128-cell or plug trays suit herbs and flowers better. Match your cell size to your typical seed-starting needs.
  • Durable material that heats well: Rigid plastic trays outlast flimsy alternatives by years. Look for thick polystyrene or polypropylene that won't crack after a few seasons in storage. Additionally, choose darker-colored trays (black or dark green) because they absorb warmth from grow lights better, maintaining the soil temperature seedlings prefer (65-75°F).

What to Avoid

  • Overly deep trays without drainage: Trays that are 4+ inches deep without proper drainage holes encourage root rot and damping-off disease. Shallow trays (1-1.5 inches) with excellent drainage are far superior for the typical 6-8 week indoor growing period.
  • Very thin, flimsy plastic: Discount trays made from thin plastic warp when exposed to heat from grow lights, become brittle in storage, and degrade quickly. They typically last only one season, making them expensive long-term compared to quality options.
  • Trays without humidity control: Starting seeds in uncovered trays requires daily misting or watering, which can wash away small seeds and spread disease. Without a dome or cover, you'll also deal with excessive evaporation and inconsistent soil moisture that leads to germination failures.
