What to Look For

  • Drainage capability: Large planters must have drainage holes and ideally a drainage layer to prevent root rot. Look for planters with multiple drainage points, especially those designed with built-in saucers that catch excess water without pooling around the roots.
  • Material durability and weight: For outdoor conditions, consider UV-resistant resin, high-quality ceramic, or treated wood that won't deteriorate in sun and rain. Weight matters too—a planter heavy enough to stay stable in wind but not so heavy you can't relocate it for seasonal adjustments.
  • Size and depth for root systems: Large plants need adequate soil depth; aim for at least 18-24 inches deep for most perennials and shrubs. The wider the planter, the more stable it becomes and the more room roots have to establish strong growth.

Our Top Pick

We recommend resin composite planters in the 20-24 inch range for most outdoor gardening situations. These lightweight yet durable containers offer exceptional value for serious gardeners. They're UV-resistant, won't crack in freeze-thaw cycles like ceramic can, and they're genuinely affordable compared to quality ceramic or stone options. Models with integrated drainage systems and saucer bases keep your plants healthy while protecting your patio or deck from water damage.

Why This Works for This Situation

Large outdoor plants demand consistent moisture and nutrient availability, which only happens with sufficient soil volume. A 20-24 inch planter holds roughly 30-50 gallons of soil, providing thermal mass that keeps roots cooler in summer and warmer in winter. This larger soil volume also means less frequent watering—an especially important feature during hot months when shallow containers dry out daily.

Resin composite materials have revolutionized outdoor gardening because they mimic the look of stone or ceramic while solving their real-world problems. They won't shatter if frozen, won't become brittle after years of UV exposure, and they're light enough to move around your garden space as your planting plans evolve. If you're using drip irrigation or soaker systems, these planters pair perfectly because their consistent material won't develop cracks where water escapes unpredictably.

What to Avoid

  • Untreated wood or thin plastic: Untreated wood deteriorates quickly outdoors and can harbor pests, while thin plastic becomes brittle and cracks within a season or two. These materials simply won't provide the longevity you need for a serious planting investment.
  • Planters without drainage: Any large planter without proper drainage holes will create waterlogged soil that kills plants. Decorative planters without drainage should only be used as outer containers for smaller pots inside them.
  • Oversized planters for small plants: While "go big" seems logical, a tiny plant in a 30-inch planter leads to soggy soil and slow growth. Match planter size to your plant's mature root system—typically 2-4 inches larger in diameter than the plant's current root ball.

Your outdoor garden deserves planters that work as hard as you do—ones that drain properly, withstand the elements, and give your plants room to thrive. Frequently Asked Questions

What size planter do I need for large outdoor plants?

For most large outdoor plants, use containers at least 18-24 inches deep and wide, though trees and shrubs may need 24-36 inches or larger. A good rule is to choose a planter that's about one-third the height of your mature plant. Ensure the pot is proportional to the plant's root system—larger plants with spreading roots need more width than depth.

What material is best for large outdoor planters?

Terracotta is attractive but heavy and porous; it dries out quickly but is durable. Fiberglass and resin are lightweight and weather-resistant, ideal for large planters you'll need to move. Concrete and ceramic are sturdy and long-lasting but extremely heavy, while composite materials offer a good balance of durability and weight.

How do I prevent large planters from cracking in winter?

Choose frost-resistant materials like fiberglass, resin, or high-fired ceramic rather than low-fired terracotta. If using terracotta, bring pots indoors during freezing weather or wrap them with insulation. Always ensure proper drainage so water doesn't collect and freeze inside the planter, which causes cracking.

Do large outdoor planters need drainage holes?

Yes, drainage holes are essential to prevent root rot and standing water, even for outdoor plants. If a decorative planter lacks drainage, use it as a cache pot by placing a draining pot inside it. Without drainage, most plants will develop fungal issues and decline within weeks.
