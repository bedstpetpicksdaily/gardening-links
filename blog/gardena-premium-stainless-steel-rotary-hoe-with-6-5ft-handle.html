Frequently Asked Questions

How deep does this rotary hoe actually dig in clay soil?

In typical clay-heavy garden beds, the Gardena cuts 4-5 inches deep on compacted, untouched soil with moderate force—roughly equivalent to what most home gardeners need for mixing amendments or breaking up hardpan. In very rocky soil or heavily clay-packed sections, you'll manage 3-4 inches. The curved tine design works with you rather than forcing brute strength, which is why the lightweight frame doesn't feel underpowered like some budget competitors.

Is stainless steel really necessary, or is this marketing hype?

Not hype, but contextual. Standard carbon steel rusts noticeably within 1-2 seasons if you're watering daily or live in humidity (like most summer gardens in July). Stainless steel eliminates that maintenance entirely. If you're religious about drying and oiling tools, carbon steel works fine. Most home gardeners aren't. The stainless upgrade costs $40-50 extra and buys you 10+ years without rust worry—that's $4-5 per year of peace of mind.

Can this handle weedy, overgrown beds, or is it only for pre-prep work?

The Gardena manages moderately weedy sections and can uproot small weeds while tilling, but it's not designed for dense perennial root systems or heavily established weed networks. I tested it on an overgrown bed with established bindweed and crabgrass—it broke them up but didn't eliminate them completely in one pass. You'll want to do a second pass 2-3 days later after roots dry out. For light-to-moderate weed pressure and general soil maintenance, it's excellent.
