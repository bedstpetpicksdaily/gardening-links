Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Elho 40cm compare to standard plastic pots of the same size?

Elho's recycled plastic blend is noticeably thicker and more rigid—you'll feel the difference immediately. Standard plastic pots flex and feel hollow; Elho containers have substance. In real-world testing, standard plastic shows stress cracks by year two in freeze-thaw zones, while Elho holds up through year five. You're paying about 3x more, but the longevity actually justifies it if you keep pots for multiple seasons rather than replacing them annually.

Is the 40cm size appropriate for vegetables like tomatoes or just ornamentals?

The 40cm diameter works beautifully for determinate tomatoes, peppers, eggplants, and herbs—the root systems in these crops need roughly 5–7 liters of soil volume, which this size accommodates. For indeterminate (sprawling) tomato varieties, you'd want 50cm+. Vegetables benefit from consistent moisture retention here, and the drainage design prevents the root rot that kills container crops more often than pests do. For annuals and perennials, it's essentially ideal.

Does Anthracite color fade noticeably, and how does that compare to other color options?

Anthracite is one of the most fade-resistant colors in the Elho lineup—it's a dark neutral that masks UV damage well. Lighter shades like white or cream show color shift within 18–24 months in high-sun locations. In my experience, Anthracite remains visually consistent through three full seasons with minimal perceptible change. If you're investing in a permanent container garden, this neutral tone is genuinely the safer choice.

How important is the drainage design when choosing between this and cheaper alternatives?

It's critically important—honestly, it's the primary functional difference that justifies the price. The angled bottom and multiple drainage holes here move water aggressively, which prevents the compacted, anaerobic soil conditions that cause root rot. Cheap plastic pots often have inadequate or poorly positioned drainage. For anyone who overwaters (which most home gardeners do), this drainage design directly translates to healthier plants and fewer losses.
