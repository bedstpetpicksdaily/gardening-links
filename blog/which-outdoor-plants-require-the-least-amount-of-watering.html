Which Outdoor Plants Require the Least Amount of Watering?

Succulents, sedums, lavender, Russian sage, and ornamental grasses are outdoor plants that require the least watering—many thriving on rainfall alone once established. These drought-tolerant varieties have adapted to retain water and can go weeks or months without supplemental irrigation.

The Short Answer

Drought-tolerant plants like sedums, agave, lavender, and ornamental grasses are your best bet for minimal watering. These species have evolved to store water in their leaves or roots, making them extremely efficient in dry conditions. Once established (typically after one growing season), most require water only during extended dry spells. This makes them ideal for busy gardeners, water-conscious homeowners, and arid climates.

The Full Explanation

Understanding which plants need minimal water starts with recognizing their natural adaptations. Plants that require the least watering share specific characteristics: thick, waxy leaves; deep root systems; or specialized water-storage tissues.

Top Drought-Tolerant Plant Categories

  • Succulents: Sedums, echeveria, and aloe store water in their fleshy leaves. They need water only every 2-3 weeks during growing season, and can go dormant in winter with no irrigation.
  • Ornamental Grasses: Fountain grass, blue fescue, and miscanthus have deep roots that access moisture deep in soil. They typically need water only during establishment and severe droughts.
  • Mediterranean Herbs: Lavender, rosemary, thyme, and oregano evolved in low-rainfall regions. They prefer dry conditions and may actually suffer from overwatering.
  • Shrubs and Small Trees: Desert rose, Russian sage, butterfly bush, and dwarf Alberta spruce require minimal supplemental water once their roots establish.
  • Groundcovers: Sedum varieties, creeping thyme, and ice plant spread across soil while needing minimal water input.

The key to success with drought-tolerant plants is proper establishment. During the first growing season, water regularly to help roots develop deep into the soil. After establishment, reduce watering frequency significantly. Most drought-tolerant plants prefer "soak and dry" conditions—thoroughly water, then let soil dry out completely before watering again.

Soil composition matters enormously. Drought-tolerant plants thrive in well-draining soil. If your garden has heavy clay, amend it with sand, gravel, or compost to improve drainage. Poor drainage can kill drought-tolerant plants faster than drought itself, since they're vulnerable to root rot.

What the Experts Say

Horticulturists and water conservation specialists consistently recommend xeriscaping—landscaping designed to reduce water use—as the most sustainable approach to outdoor gardening. The EPA and local water authorities across the country promote drought-tolerant plants as essential to water conservation. Expert consensus emphasizes that establishing drought-tolerant plants requires patience during year one, but pays dividends for years afterward. Many professional landscapers now design primarily with these species, recognizing both environmental benefits and reduced maintenance costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What outdoor plants need watering once a week or less?

Sedums, lavender, Russian sage, and ornamental grasses are excellent choices that thrive on minimal water once established. These drought-tolerant plants typically need deep watering only once weekly or even less frequently during cooler months, making them ideal for low-maintenance gardens.

Which low-water outdoor plants survive hot, dry climates?

Agave, desert rose, blanket flower, and yucca are specifically adapted to arid conditions and can survive extended periods without supplemental watering. Once their root systems establish in 2-3 months, they often require water only during extreme heat waves or prolonged droughts.

Can outdoor shrubs go weeks without watering?

Yes, mature drought-resistant shrubs like Texas privet, Texas ash, and junipers can go 2-3 weeks without watering in moderate climates. These woody plants develop deep root systems that access moisture in lower soil layers, though newly planted shrubs will need weekly watering for the first growing season.

What ground cover plants need the least water outdoors?

Creeping thyme, sedum varieties, and bearberry are ground covers that require minimal watering once established. These spreading plants are perfect for slopes and sunny areas where they naturally suppress weeds while needing water only during extended dry spells lasting more than 3-4 weeks.
