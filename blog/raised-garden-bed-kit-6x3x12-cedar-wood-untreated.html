Frequently Asked Questions

How long does untreated cedar actually last in the ground?

Based on customer reviews and industry standards, untreated western red cedar typically lasts 10-15 years when the boards make direct contact with soil. The bottom boards degrade first—expect 8-10 years of solid performance before you see noticeable wood softening at the base. Damp climates accelerate this; arid regions see longer lifespans. Staining or sealing every 2-3 years can extend it another 3-5 years.

Is this cedar bed cheaper than pressure-treated, or am I just paying for looks?

You're paying for both. Pressure-treated wood typically costs $40-80 for a comparable bed and lasts 8-12 years. Cedar runs $100-200+ but avoids chemical treatment and generally looks better longer (if maintained). For pure budget value, pressure-treated wins. For peace of mind growing vegetables organically and long-term aesthetic appeal, cedar's premium is justified—just not by a huge margin.

Do I need to treat or seal the cedar, or does 'untreated' mean maintenance-free?

Untreated cedar is maintenance-free in terms of preventing rot, but it's not weathering-free. Without sealing, expect the wood to turn gray within 12-24 months. If you want to preserve the natural cedar color, apply a water-repellent sealant every 2-3 years—roughly a 2-hour project and $20-40 in materials per application. Most gardeners either embrace the weathered look or commit to occasional maintenance. Leaving it bare is fine; it just won't look 'new' for long.

How does this 6x3x12 size compare to other popular dimensions?

The 6x3 footprint (18 sq ft) is ideal for small-to-medium gardens. A 4x8 bed gives you 32 sq ft for similar or less cost—better ROI if you need volume. An 8x2 is narrow and awkward to reach across. For the 6x3, the 12-inch depth is adequate for greens, herbs, and shallow-rooted crops but tight for root vegetables. If you're growing carrots, parsnips, or deep-rooting tomatoes, consider 15-18 inch depth. This kit is versatile but not ideal for every crop type.
